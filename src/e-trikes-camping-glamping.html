Bringing Grandma and Grandpa Along: E-Trikes for Camping & Glamping

July 10, 2026 7 min read Camping

The Part of the Trip Nobody Plans For

Most camping and van life planning revolves around power, water, and where to park for the night. One thing that's easy to overlook: how far everything actually is once you get there. A "quick walk" to the bathhouse, the lake, or the lodge can be 10-15 minutes each way on gravel or packed dirt — manageable for most of us, a lot less fun for a grandparent with knees that don't love uneven ground.

If you've ever tried to bring an older relative along on a camping trip or a glamping weekend, you've probably run into this: they want to come, they want to be part of it, but the amount of walking involved quietly becomes the reason they sit it out. An e-trike is one of the simpler fixes for that specific problem.

Why a Trike, Not a Bike or Scooter

Two wheels ask for balance. A mobility scooter asks for a curb-free, paved world that most campgrounds don't have. A three-wheeled e-trike sits in between: it doesn't require balancing to stay upright, and the wider stance and bigger tires handle gravel, grass, and dirt paths a lot better than a scooter's small wheels.

  • No balance required — the trike stays upright on its own, even stopped.
  • Step-through frame — easy to get on and off without swinging a leg over a high bar.
  • Throttle option — no pedaling required if knees or stamina are a concern, though most models support pedal-assist too.
  • Storage basket — enough room for a water bottle, a camp chair cushion, or the day's book.

Worth saying plainly: an e-trike is a comfort and mobility convenience, not a medical mobility aid. If a family member has a diagnosed mobility condition, balance issue, or is currently using a cane, walker, or wheelchair, check with their doctor or physical therapist before assuming a trike is the right fit for them.

What to Look For Before Buying

Step-through height

The lower the step-through frame, the easier it is for someone with limited hip or knee flexibility to get on and off. This matters more than almost any spec sheet number.

Throttle vs. pedal-assist

A throttle-only mode means less physical effort, good for longer distances or hot afternoons. Pedal-assist is nice to have for relatives who want some light exercise but appreciate a boost on inclines.

Range for a weekend

You don't need a 60-mile range for campground use — most sites are small enough that 15-20 miles of range covers a full day of back-and-forth trips. But check whether the campground has outlets near your site to top off the battery overnight.

Folding and storage

If you're towing it on a van, truck bed, or hitch rack, a folding frame or quick-release wheels make a real difference versus wrestling a full-size trike in and out.

Making It Work at the Campsite

  • Check the campground's rules first. Some resorts restrict e-bikes/e-trikes to paved paths only, or require a speed cap — worth a quick call before you pack it.
  • Charge it the night before you leave if you're relying on your own power setup, so it's topped off before you're off-grid.
  • Scout the route once without your relative before their first ride, so you know which paths are actually trike-friendly.
  • Bring it to the golf-cart-only resorts too — most glamping resorts that restrict cars still allow e-trikes on their internal paths.

The Bottom Line

The goal isn't turning your camping trip into an e-bike showcase — it's removing the one obstacle that quietly keeps older relatives from wanting to come along. A stable, low-step, easy-throttle trike solves the "it's too far to walk" problem without asking anyone to relearn balance or navigate a scooter through gravel. If that's the thing standing between grandma and grandpa and a weekend at the lake with everyone else, it's a small investment for a trip they can actually be part of.
